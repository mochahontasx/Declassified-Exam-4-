CH. 36 — LOSS, GRIEF & DYING

Grief is theory + safety + dignity

Types of loss

  • Actual — recognized by others (death, amputation).
  • Perceived — internal, unique to the person (loss of identity, independence).
  • Maturational — normal developmental transitions (leaving home, aging).
  • Situational — sudden, unpredictable (accident, job loss).
  • Anticipatory — grieving before the loss happens.

The grief theorists

  • Kübler-Ross — 5 stages: Denial, Anger, Bargaining, Depression, Acceptance.
  • Bowlby — phases: Numbing → Yearning/Searching → Disorganization/Despair → Reorganization.
  • Worden — active TASKS: accept reality → work through pain → adjust → relocate & move forward.
  • Engel — Shock/disbelief → Developing awareness → Restitution (funeral rituals).

Complicated grief subtypes

  • Chronic — intense, doesn't fade over years.
  • Delayed — postponed, triggered later.
  • Masked — physical symptoms; person doesn't link them to loss.
  • Exaggerated — self-destructive (phobias, suicidal ideation).
  • Disenfranchised — loss not socially validated.

Signs of imminent death

  • Mottling, cool/cyanotic extremities.
  • Cheyne-Stokes / agonal breathing; "death rattle" (secretions).
  • ↓ urine output, ↓ appetite, ↓ LOC.
  • Hearing is the LAST sense to go — always speak kindly.

Palliative vs hospice

  • Palliative — comfort at ANY stage, alongside curative care.
  • Hospice — terminal, ≤6 months, curative care stops.
  • Both are interdisciplinary and comfort-focused.

Legal & postmortem

  • Living will = treatment preferences. Health care proxy/DPOA = names a person. POLST = current medical orders.
  • Directives activate ONLY when the patient lacks capacity.
  • Elevate HOB 15–30° after death (prevents facial lividity); dentures in; ID tags on toe + shroud.
  • Autopsy? Leave all tubes/lines in place.
Tested traps: "I know exactly how you feel" = non-therapeutic (shifts focus to nurse). A valid DNR is honored over family pressure. Brain death = legally dead even with a ventilator-maintained heartbeat. Don't force food on the dying — decreased appetite is normal.
CH. 37 — STRESS & COPING

From neuro wiring to defense mechanisms

The stress wiring

  • Medulla oblongata — controls HR, BP, respirations.
  • Reticular formation — monitors status, rouses you from sleep.
  • Pituitary — releases ACTH → adrenal cortex → cortisol.
  • Adrenal medulla → epinephrine/norepinephrine.

GAS (Selye) stages

  • Alarm — fight-or-flight: ↑HR, ↑glucose, dilated pupils, ↓digestion, ↓immunity.
  • Resistance — body compensates; hormones return toward baseline.
  • Exhaustion — response intensifies again, then defenses fail.
  • Allostatic load = chronic wear & tear → HTN, autoimmune, fatigue.

Appraisal & coping

  • Primary appraisal — "Is this a threat?"
  • Secondary appraisal — "What can I do? Who can help?"
  • Problem-focused — change the stressor (study more).
  • Emotion-focused — manage feelings (walk, vent).

Defense mechanisms

  • Sublimation — MOST adaptive: energy → constructive outlet.
  • Displacement — emotion onto a safer target.
  • Projection — your faults blamed on others.
  • Regression / Denial / Suppression (conscious) vs Repression (unconscious).

Crisis types

  • Maturational — predictable milestones.
  • Situational — personal external event.
  • Adventitious — disasters/mass casualties.
  • Resolves in ~6 weeks; safety is the first priority.

Nurse hazards

  • Burnout — exhaustion, cynicism, ineffectiveness.
  • Secondary traumatic stress — intrusive thoughts, nightmares.
  • Compassion fatigue = burnout + STS.
  • Second victim — trauma after being part of an error.
Tested traps: Severe anxiety → stay with patient, reduce stimuli, one-step directions (never complex teaching). Mild anxiety is the BEST state for learning. Anaerobic metabolism by-product = lactic acid. Objective findings = what the nurse observes; subjective = what the patient reports.
CH. 42 — FLUIDS, ELECTROLYTES & ACID-BASE

The numbers + the killer rules

IV fluid tonicity

  • Isotonic (0.9% NaCl, LR) — expands volume; first for hypovolemia/hemorrhage.
  • Hypotonic (0.45% NaCl) — into cells; NEVER in cerebral edema.
  • Hypertonic (3% NaCl) — for severe symptomatic hyponatremia; central line, slow.
  • D5W — isotonic in bag, hypotonic in body; never for resuscitation or to prime blood.

Potassium (3.5–5.0)

  • Hyperkalemia — peaked T-waves, wide QRS → calcium gluconate FIRST (cardiac protection).
  • Hypokalemia — U-waves, weakness, ileus.
  • IV KCl NEVER pushed — dilute, pump, ≤10 mEq/hr, cardiac monitor.

Calcium & magnesium

  • Hypocalcemia — Trousseau & Chvostek, prolonged QT, tetany.
  • Hypercalcemia — lethargy, ↓reflexes, stones, short QT.
  • Ca & phosphorus are inverse.
  • Low Mg mimics low Ca (hyperexcitable); calcium gluconate is the Mg-toxicity antidote.

Sodium (135–145)

  • Hyponatremia — confusion, seizures (water problem).
  • Hypernatremia — thirst, dry membranes.
  • Correct sodium SLOWLY — fast = osmotic demyelination / cerebral edema.

ABGs — use ROME

  • Respiratory Opposite, Metabolic Equal.
  • pH 7.35–7.45 · PaCO₂ 35–45 · HCO₃ 22–26.
  • Normal pH + abnormal values = compensated.
  • Vomiting/NG suction → metabolic alkalosis; diarrhea → metabolic acidosis.

Transfusion reactions

  • Hemolytic — flank/back pain, chills, ↓BP → STOP immediately, run NS.
  • Febrile — fever/chills (most common).
  • Allergic — itching/hives.
  • 1 kg weight = ~1 L fluid; daily weights are the best balance monitor.
Tested traps: Calcium gluconate goes FIRST in hyperkalemia. Potassium is NEVER IV push. 3% saline needs a central line + slow rate + neuro checks. D5W never primes blood. Question any order that worsens an already-extreme value (3% saline at Na 160).
CH. 46 — URINARY ELIMINATION

Flow, function, and CAUTI safety

Kidney functions

  • Flow: kidney → ureters → bladder → urethra.
  • Erythropoietin → RBCs (CKD → anemia).
  • Activates vitamin D (calcium/bone).
  • Renin → blood pressure (RAAS).

Urine volume terms

  • Anuria — <50 mL/24h (per your plan).
  • Oliguria — <400 mL/day.
  • Polyuria — >2.5 L/day.
  • Dysuria (painful), nocturia (night), hematuria (blood).

Incontinence

  • Stress — leak with cough/laugh → Kegels.
  • Urge — sudden strong urge.
  • Overflow — overfull bladder dribbles (BPH).
  • Functional — barriers reaching the toilet.
  • Reflex — spinal cord injury.

Bladder scan / PVR

  • Normal PVR <50 mL · borderline 50–200 · abnormal >200 · severe 400–500.
  • Female + hysterectomy → set scanner to MALE.
  • Drainage bag ALWAYS below bladder, off side rails.

AKI types

  • Prerenal — ↓blood flow (sepsis, dehydration, HF).
  • Intrarenal — direct kidney tissue damage.
  • Postrenal — obstruction (stones, BPH).

Meds & contrast

  • Phenazopyridine — orange urine (analgesic, not antibiotic).
  • Bethanechol — cholinergic, promotes emptying.
  • Anticholinergics/cold meds — worsen BPH retention.
  • Push fluids after contrast (nephrotoxic); watch for contrast allergy after IVP.
Tested traps: Output 15–20 mL/hr (oliguria) → notify provider, don't just flush. Break in sterile technique during catheter insertion → start over with a new catheter. T6+ SCI with severe headache & BP 200/110 = autonomic dysreflexia → sit UPRIGHT first, then find the trigger (blocked catheter). Dark purple/black stoma = ischemia → notify immediately.
CH. 47 — BOWEL ELIMINATION

GI tract, ostomies, tubes & enemas

Assessment order

  • Abdomen = Inspect → Auscultate → Percuss → Palpate (IAPP).
  • Auscultate BEFORE palpating (palpation alters bowel sounds).
  • Hyperactive = every 2–3 sec; absent = none in 5 min.

Ostomy output

  • Ileostomy — always liquid; high dehydration/electrolyte risk; enzymes damage skin.
  • Sigmoid colostomy — formed; can be irrigated for control.
  • Stoma should be pink/red & moist; purple/black = emergency.
  • Cut wafer 1/8 inch larger than stoma.

NG tubes

  • Measure: nose → earlobe → xiphoid.
  • X-ray = gold standard placement; pH ≤5.5 suggests gastric.
  • Air-bolus auscultation is outdated/unreliable.
  • Salem sump blue pigtail = air vent → instill air if leaking.

Enemas

  • Position: left lateral Sims', right knee flexed.
  • Cramping → lower the bag (slow flow).
  • Tap water (hypotonic) → water toxicity risk; Fleet risky in renal failure.
  • Oil-retention enema FIRST for impaction (retain 30–60 min).

Constipation / impaction

  • Liquid stool oozing around a hard mass = impaction.
  • Opioids are the biggest impaction risk (slow peristalsis).
  • Digital removal can trigger vagal bradycardia → stop, assess.
  • Bowel retraining: attempt ~1 hr after a meal (gastrocolic reflex).

Screening & stool

  • Colorectal screening starts at 45 (2018 ACS).
  • gFOBT — avoid vitamin C / red meat. FIT — no diet restrictions.
  • Clay/white stool = no bile; black/tarry = upper GI/iron; bright red = lower GI bleed.
  • C. diff → soap & water (alcohol won't kill spores).
Tested traps: Constipation then sudden watery stool → assess for impaction FIRST (don't give antidiarrheals). HOB 30–45° for tube feedings (aspiration). Purple/black stoma = ischemia → notify. Bradycardia during digital disimpaction → stop the procedure.

💗 Chapter 36 — Loss & Grief

Types of Loss · Grief Theories · Advance Directives · Palliative vs. Hospice · End-of-Life Nursing

🎚️ Stage Simulator — walk through DABDA

Drag to move a patient through each Kübler-Ross stage. The face, the mood, and the nurse's therapeutic move all change with you.

Denial
DenialAngerBargainDepressAccept
Denial: the patient refuses or avoids the reality of the diagnosis to protect themselves.
⚠️ TRICK WORDS: "FIRST response" to denial/anger = therapeutic communication (acknowledge feelings). "BEST action" DNR + family wants CPR = honor the DNR. Advance directive only activates when patient CANNOT make decisions. NEVER say "I know how you feel."

📚 4 Types of Loss

1. Physical/Actual Loss

Tangible, verifiable. Death, amputation, job, divorce, pet.

2. Maturational Loss

Natural development milestone. Child leaves home, retirement, menopause. Often dismissed — grief is REAL.

3. Situational Loss

Unpredictable external event. Sudden illness, accident. Disrupts safety.

4. Perceived/Disenfranchised Loss

NOT recognized by others. High-risk of invalidation. Nurse ALWAYS validates. Identity loss with illness, secret relationships.

🧠 Grief Theories

Denial → Anger → Bargaining → Depression → Acceptance
⚠️ NOT linear. Never push a patient through stages.

Denial: "Tell me what you understand about your condition." Never say "But the tests confirm it."
Anger: May be directed at you. Acknowledge without reacting: "It sounds like you're really frustrated."
Bargaining: "If I pray harder..." — Listen. Don't promise what you can't give.
Depression: Sit silently. "It's okay to feel this way." Don't rush.
Acceptance: Support autonomy and dignity.
1. Numbness/Protest (shock)
2. Yearning & Searching (looking for what was lost)
3. Disorganization/Despair (confusion, sadness)
4. Reorganization (reinvesting in life)

📝 Bowlby = PHASES. Focused on attachment loss.
1. Accept the reality of the loss
2. Work through the pain of grief
3. Adjust to environment without deceased
4. Find enduring connection while embarking on new life

📝 Worden = TASKS (active — what the person DOES). Differs from Kübler-Ross stages.

📋 Advance Directives — 6 Types

Living Will

Written — what treatment patient wants/refuses if incapacitated.

DPAHC

Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare — names a surrogate decision-maker. ≠ financial POA.

DNR

No CPR if arrest. Must be physician-signed. Nurse honors regardless of family pressure.

POLST/MOLST

Physician ORDER (more binding than directive). Goes with patient everywhere.

Healthcare Proxy/Surrogate

Person designated to decide when patient cannot.

Organ Donation

Specific documentation required. Notify OPO. Never pressure family.
⚠️ NCLEX TRAP: Advance directive only ACTIVATES when patient CANNOT make decisions. If patient can currently decide → their current voice OVERRIDES the document.

🕯️ End-of-Life Physical Signs

Cheyne-Stokes
Irregular breathing with apnea pauses. Normal at EOL.
Death Rattle
Secretions in throat. Suction PRN, reposition, explain to family.
Mottling

Hands/feet cool then cyanotic. Normal sign of imminent death.

🔓 Why Grief Matters in Nursing

Every patient you'll ever care for is grieving something — a diagnosis, a lost function, a lost future. When someone gets a cancer diagnosis, they grieve the life they thought they'd have. Your job isn't to fix grief — it's to witness it without flinching. That's harder than it sounds.

💡 Why Kübler-Ross Matters on NCLEX

The stages exist so you understand where a patient is emotionally — NOT so you can push them forward. The NCLEX tests whether you respond therapeutically at each stage. Patient in denial? Don't rip that away. Patient who's angry at you? That anger is pain looking for somewhere to go. Take the hit without throwing one back.

📋 Why Advance Directives Matter

People make these documents when they CAN so their wishes are honored when they CAN'T. The NCLEX loves testing what happens when family and patient disagree — the patient's documented wishes almost always win. The document only activates when the patient cannot currently make decisions.

AlarmResistanceExhaustion
Alarm: fight-or-flight kicks in — catecholamines and cortisol surge; HR and BP rise.
⚠️ TRICK WORDS: "Moderate anxiety" = learning IS still possible with guidance. "Severe anxiety" = cannot problem-solve at all. "Panic" = STAY with patient, do NOT leave. Sublimation = MOST ADAPTIVE defense mechanism. Suppression (conscious) ≠ Repression (unconscious).

⚡ GAS — General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye)

Stage 1: ALARM

Fight-or-flight. ↑HR, ↑BP, ↑RR, ↑cortisol, ↑blood glucose. Pupils dilate. Blood shunts to muscles. Body mobilizes ALL resources.

Stage 2: RESISTANCE

Body tries to adapt. Hormones stay elevated. Can maintain for a while — but costs energy. Chronic stress begins here.

Stage 3: EXHAUSTION

Resources depleted. Body can no longer fight. Illness, organ damage, immune suppression — even death. Chronic stress = chronic illness.

😰 Peplau's 4 Anxiety Levels — KNOW EACH!

1. MILD (+1)

Heightened awareness. Motivated, focused. Can learn well. Examples: studying for exam, first day at work.

2. MODERATE (+2)

Perceptual field narrows. Misses details. Slight tremors, voice changes. LEARNING IS STILL POSSIBLE with guidance and direction.

3. SEVERE (+3)

Focus on ONE detail only. Cannot problem-solve. Headache, palpitations, diarrhea. Cannot learn. Needs simple, direct, one-step directions.

4. PANIC (+4)

Total disorganization. Hallucinations, dissociation, loss of reality. Flight, immobility, or rage. STAY WITH PATIENT. Do NOT leave alone. Ensure safety FIRST.
💡 NCLEX FIRST action for severe/panic anxiety → Stay with patient, speak slowly and calmly, use simple words, reduce stimuli, ensure safety. Then notify provider if needed.

🚨 Types of Crisis

Maturational Crisis

Expected life transition — marriage, parenthood, retirement, menopause. Person lacks coping resources for the change.

Situational Crisis

Unexpected external event — illness, job loss, divorce, accident. Sudden and overwhelming.

Adventitious Crisis

Natural disaster, terrorism, mass casualty. Affects whole communities. Nurse: meet BASIC NEEDS first (Maslow).
💡 Crisis intervention GOAL = Return to PRE-CRISIS level of function (not better, not fixed — just baseline). Acute crisis resolves in 4–6 weeks.

🔓 Why GAS Matters

Selye discovered your body responds to ALL stressors the same way — whether it's running from a bear or worrying about rent. Alarm mobilizes everything. Resistance tries to maintain it. Exhaustion is when the body collapses. Chronic stress = your immune system tanks = you get sick. That's why stressed students get sicker during exam week.

🔓 Defense Mechanisms — Not Always Bad

Defense mechanisms are your unconscious mind protecting you from overwhelming anxiety. Denial right after a traumatic diagnosis? Your brain is buying time. Short-term, it can be protective. Long-term, it can stop you from getting treatment. As a nurse, don't rip defenses away — gently work alongside them.

⚠️ TRICK WORDS: Respiratory = ROME (opposite = Respiratory Opposite). Metabolic = SAME (same direction). Compensation: respiratory compensates FAST (minutes), kidneys compensate SLOW (days). D5W = isotonic in the BAG but hypotonic in the BODY once glucose metabolized.

🩸 ABG Normal Values — Memorize These!

pH

Normal: 7.35–7.45
<7.35 = ACIDOSIS
>7.45 = ALKALOSIS

PaCO₂ (Respiratory)

Normal: 35–45 mmHg
>45 = respiratory ACIDOSIS
<35 = respiratory ALKALOSIS

HCO₃⁻ (Metabolic)

Normal: 22–26 mEq/L
<22 = metabolic ACIDOSIS
>26 = metabolic ALKALOSIS

PaO₂

Normal: 80–100 mmHg
SpO₂ normal: 95–100%

🧠 ROME Mnemonic

Respiratory Opposite  |  Metabolic Equal
Respiratory OPPOSITE: pH↑ + CO₂↓ = Resp Alkalosis | pH↓ + CO₂↑ = Resp Acidosis
Metabolic EQUAL: pH↑ + HCO₃↑ = Met Alkalosis | pH↓ + HCO₃↓ = Met Acidosis

⚡ 4 Key Electrolytes

Hyponatremia (<135): Cells SWELL (water moves IN). Signs: headache, confusion, seizures, nausea. Tx: fluid restriction, hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) for severe cases via CENTRAL LINE — correct SLOWLY (risk of osmotic demyelination syndrome if too fast).

Hypernatremia (>145): Cells SHRINK (water moves OUT). Signs: excessive thirst, dry mucous membranes, ↑HR, confusion, seizures. Tx: free water replacement (D5W or 0.45% NaCl) — correct SLOWLY.
Hypokalemia (<3.5): Signs: muscle weakness, fatigue, cramps, constipation, ↓DTRs, U-waves on EKG, paralytic ileus. FATAL ARRHYTHMIA risk!
Tx: PO or IV potassium. IV K⁺ NEVER IV push (cardiac arrest!) — always diluted, with cardiac monitoring.

Hyperkalemia (>5.0): Signs: peaked T-waves on EKG, muscle weakness, bradycardia → cardiac arrest. Causes: renal failure, acidosis, K⁺-sparing diuretics.
Tx: Kayexalate, insulin + dextrose (drives K into cells), calcium gluconate (cardiac protection), dialysis.
⚠️ NEVER give IV potassium as a bolus — it will STOP the heart.
Hypocalcemia (<9): CATS — Convulsions, Arrhythmias, Tetany, Spasm/Stridor. Positive Chvostek sign (tap face → twitching) and Trousseau sign (inflate BP cuff → carpal spasm).
Tx: IV calcium gluconate, dietary calcium, vitamin D.

Hypercalcemia (>10.5): BONES GROANS STONES MOANS — bone pain, GI distress, kidney stones, psychiatric moans (confusion). Tx: fluids, furosemide, bisphosphonates.
Hypomagnesemia (<1.5): Neuro irritability, tremors, confusion, seizures. Often accompanies hypokalemia and hypocalcemia. Causes: alcoholism, malnutrition, diarrhea.

Hypermagnesemia (>2.5): ↓DTRs, ↓BP, ↓RR, bradycardia, cardiac arrest. Causes: excessive Mg antacids/laxatives, renal failure.
Antidote: Calcium gluconate (for Mg toxicity).

🩸 Blood Transfusion Safety

Before Transfusion

TWO-NURSE verification: Name, DOB, blood type, unit number, expiration date. Never ask "Are you Mrs. Jones?" — ask patient to state name. Obtain baseline VS.

Starting & Monitoring

Start slowly: 2 mL/min × first 15 min. Stay at bedside. VS: q15 min × first hour. Use 18G+ catheter. Only prime with NORMAL SALINE (never D5W — causes hemolysis).

Transfusion Reactions

Acute Hemolytic (MOST SEVERE): STOP immediately. Keep IV open with NS. Notify provider STAT. Send urine. Never catch up on delayed infusions.

Reaction Signs

Fever, chills, flank/back pain, dyspnea, hives, ↓BP. First sign = STOP. First action = STOP transfusion. Then maintain IV access with NS.

HOT 3IV fluid selection — isotonic vs. hypotonic vs. hypertonic based on patient scenario
HOT 4Blood transfusion reaction → STOP first, maintain IV with NS, notify provider
HOT 5Hypocalcemia signs: CATS + Chvostek + Trousseau. Antidote for Mg toxicity = calcium gluconate

🔓 Why ABGs Matter

Your blood pH must stay between 7.35–7.45 or your enzymes stop working and you die. Period. The lungs and kidneys are your emergency pH managers. When something goes wrong, they compensate — the NCLEX tests whether you can read ABGs and identify what's happening and why.

🔓 The K⁺ Tragedy

Potassium is the one electrolyte that will genuinely kill your patient fastest if managed wrong. Too low → the heart can't generate a proper beat. Too high → the heart fires uncontrollably. That's why you NEVER give IV potassium as a bolus push — it goes straight to the heart and stops it. Always diluted, always slowly, always with monitoring.

🫘 Chapter 46 — Urinary Elimination

CAUTI Bundle · 5 Incontinence Types · Catheter Insertion · Urinary Diversions · Autonomic Dysreflexia

⚠️ TRICK WORDS: Autonomic dysreflexia = EMERGENCY, FIRST action = sit patient UPRIGHT (lowers BP). CAUTI "FIRST action" = assess for need before inserting (insertion is LAST resort). Normal urine output = 30 mL/hr minimum. Urine that is clear/light yellow = GOOD.

🦠 CAUTI Prevention Bundle — 8 Rules

1. Only Insert When Necessary

Catheter use must be clinically justified. Most common reason: accurate I&O when patient critically ill.

2. Sterile Insertion Technique

Sterile gloves, sterile drape, sterile kit. One chance — if contaminated, use NEW catheter.

3. Maintain Closed Drainage System

NEVER disconnect without sterile technique. The closed system prevents organism entry.

4. Keep Bag Below Bladder

ALWAYS below bladder. NEVER on the floor. If transporting — clamp or empty first. Backflow = instant UTI risk.

5. Secure Catheter to Thigh

Prevents trauma from traction. Reduces mechanical irritation at insertion site.

6. Perineal Hygiene

Clean around catheter with soap and water. Clean toward the catheter. Never soap inside urethra.

7. Empty When 1/2–2/3 Full

Never allow overfilling — increases backpressure and infection risk. Empty into measuring container (not toilet directly).

8. Remove ASAP

Every day with catheter = ↑infection risk. Reassess need daily. Remove when no longer clinically necessary.

🚨 Autonomic Dysreflexia — EMERGENCY!

Who: Spinal cord injury at T6 or ABOVE
Trigger: Noxious stimulus BELOW level of injury (full bladder #1, fecal impaction #2, tight clothing, skin breakdown)

Signs: Pounding headache, severe hypertension (↑BP 20–40 mmHg above baseline), bradycardia, flushing ABOVE injury level, pallor BELOW, diaphoresis, nasal congestion

FIRST action = SIT PATIENT UPRIGHT (lowers BP by orthostatic effect)
Then: Check/empty catheter. Check for fecal impaction. Remove tight clothing. Notify provider. Antihypertensives if not resolved.

🔄 Urinary Diversions — Ostomy Types

Ileal Conduit (Urostomy)

Ureters → segment of ileum → stoma. Urine drains CONTINUOUSLY (no storage). Patient wears bag 24/7. Mucus in urine = NORMAL.

Stoma Assessment

Normal stoma = PINK/RED and moist. Dark/purple/black = ischemia (call provider NOW). Gray/brown = necrosis. Slight swelling initially = normal.

Continent Diversion

Internal pouch. Patient self-catheterizes at intervals. Advantages: no external bag. High-maintenance education needed.

🔓 Why CAUTI Prevention Matters

Hospital-acquired UTIs from catheters are one of the most preventable infections in healthcare. Every day a catheter stays in, bacteria climb the tube. Your technique, your vigilance, and your advocacy to remove it early literally saves lives and kidneys.

🔓 Why Autonomic Dysreflexia Is a 911

Think of it like a car alarm going off at full blast with no way to stop it. The nervous system below the SCI is screaming but the signal can't get to the brain to modulate it. Untreated, BP can spike high enough to cause a stroke, MI, or death. Sitting the patient up and removing the trigger (usually a full bladder) literally saves their life.

🌿 Chapter 47 — Bowel Elimination

Ostomy Types & Care · IAAP Priority Order · Fecal Impaction Trap · Enema Safety · NG Tube Management

⚠️ TRICK WORDS: Liquid stool in constipated patient = FECAL IMPACTION until proven otherwise (liquid seeps around impaction). "FIRST action" NG tube placement check = pH of aspirate (pH ≤5.5 = stomach). NEVER flush before confirming placement. IAAP: I=Inspect, A=Auscultate, A=Ask/Assess, P=Palpate.

🎯 IAAP — Physical Assessment Order for Abdomen

I-A-A-P (Abdominal Assessment ONLY)

Inspect — look first (color, contour, scars, visible peristalsis)
Auscultate — bowel sounds BEFORE touching (palpation changes sounds)
Ask/Assess — percussion (if applicable)
Palpate — LAST (don't disturb bowel sounds before auscultating)
⚠️ All other assessments = IPAP (Inspect → Palpate → Auscultate → Percuss). Abdomen is the ONLY exception = IAAP.

💩 Fecal Impaction — The #1 NCLEX Trap

The Trap: Patient has been constipated for days. Now they have liquid, "diarrhea-like" stool. Students pick diarrhea interventions. The answer? THIS IS FECAL IMPACTION.

Why it happens: Hard, dry stool is stuck in rectum. Liquid feces from higher up seeps AROUND the impaction and leaks out.

Assessment: Digital rectal exam → hard mass in rectum
Treatment: Oil retention enema → THEN cleansing enema. Manual removal if needed (MD/NP order required).
Prevention: Adequate fluid, fiber, activity, scheduled toileting.

🌸 Ostomies — Types & Assessment

Colostomy

Opening in COLON. Stool is solid-to-soft depending on location. Sigmoid = most formed. Ascending = most liquid. Can be temporary or permanent.

Ileostomy

Opening in ILEUM. Stool is ALWAYS liquid/watery. High risk for electrolyte imbalance (Na⁺, K⁺). Skin breakdown risk from digestive enzymes.

Stoma Colors

Pink/Red/Moist = NORMAL ✅
Pale = anemia or poor circulation ⚠️
Purple/Dark = ischemia 🚨 CALL NOW
Black/Brown = necrosis — EMERGENCY

New Ostomy Stoma

Initially swollen (normal). Pouch should be cut ⅛ inch larger than stoma. Empty when ⅓–½ full. Change entire system q3–5 days or PRN.

🔧 NG Tube — Insertion, Verification & Safety

BEST Verification Method

X-ray is GOLD STANDARD for initial placement. pH of aspirate (≤5.5 = stomach) is reliable ongoing method. NEVER air bolus auscultation — unreliable (outdated).

Before Each Feeding/Medication

Check placement via pH AND measure external tube length. Hold feeding if residual >200–250 mL (varies by protocol).

Proper Position

HOB at 30–45° during all feedings and 30–60 min after. Prevents aspiration. Check tube position before EVERY use.

Signs of Misplacement

Coughing, choking, cyanosis, ↓SpO₂ during insertion = may be in LUNG. STOP. Remove. Let patient recover. Retry.

🔓 Why Auscultate Before Palpating the Abdomen

Palpating the abdomen physically stimulates peristalsis, which falsely increases bowel sound frequency. If you palpate first, you're not hearing the patient's true baseline bowel sounds — you're hearing sounds you created. That's why the abdomen is the ONLY body system where you auscultate before you palpate.

🔓 The Fecal Impaction Trap

This is one of the sneakiest clinical presentations. Your patient says "I have diarrhea" and shows you liquid stool. Your instinct says anti-diarrheal. But if they've been constipated for days, that liquid is literally leaking AROUND a massive, hardened blockage. Give anti-diarrheal and you've just sealed the blockage tighter. Always ask: "When was your last SOLID bowel movement?"
